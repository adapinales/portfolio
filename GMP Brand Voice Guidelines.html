01 — Voice Identity
The Voice of GMP
Before writing a single caption, every piece of GMP content begins here — with a clear understanding of whose voice is speaking and what that voice carries into the room.
Voice Persona
"The woman who has done the work, is still doing the work, and speaks from inside the becoming — not above it."
The Warmth Layer — Michelle Obama
Grounded, encouraging, quietly powerful. She speaks to women as equals. There is no pedestal. There is no performance. Just a woman who believes in what another woman can become — and says so directly.
The Depth Layer — Erykah Badu
Deeply self-aware, transformational, unapologetic. She is willing to say the thing no one else is saying. She challenges conventional thinking not to be contrarian — but because she has seen something others haven't yet.
02 — Audience State
Who She's Speaking To
Every word written under The Girl Mompreneur is written for one specific woman at one specific moment in her life.
Her Emotional State When She Finds GMP
"She's figuring out who she is becoming — and realizing the life she wants will require a different version of her."
She is not lost. She is in transition. She can feel that something needs to shift but hasn't named it yet. She's seen the courses, the blueprints, the reels about passive income — and something still isn't clicking. GMP meets her exactly there, before the strategy, at the identity. The voice speaks to that woman — not the woman she was, and not the finished version either. The woman in the middle of becoming.
03 — Tone Spectrum
How the Tone Moves
GMP's voice is not one flat tone. It moves across a spectrum depending on what the content is doing — but it never loses its core character.
◦
Reflective
Used for identity and awareness content. Slower pace. Invites the reader to pause and look inward. Feels like a quiet conversation, not a broadcast.
◦
Direct
Used for truth-telling and pattern-naming content. Clear, confident, no filler. Says the thing plainly. Not harsh — but not soft either. The reader feels seen.
◦
Activating
Used for discipline and behavior content. Creates forward momentum. Leaves the reader with something to do, decide, or embody. Energizing without being hype.
04 — Format Voice Guide
Voice by Format
The voice stays consistent across formats — but how it shows up shifts based on what each format demands.
Reels — Voiceover
Tone
Intimate and unhurried. Speaks like she's telling you something she realized on her own — not performing for a camera. Pauses matter. Silence is part of the delivery.
Opening Line Rule
Never start with a question. Start with a statement that lands like a truth the viewer has been feeling but not saying. Hook through recognition, not curiosity.
Talking Head
Tone
Warm and real. Eye contact matters. Speaks to one woman, not a crowd. Personal stories are the entry point — the broader truth comes second.
Opening Line Rule
Start mid-thought or mid-realization. "I used to think..." or "Something shifted for me when..." — drop the viewer into the moment rather than setting it up.
Carousel
Tone
Clear and layered. Each slide builds on the last. No filler slides. The first slide hooks. The last slide activates. Every middle slide earns the next swipe.
Opening Line Rule
The first slide is a single declarative truth. Short. Bold. No explanation yet. The explanation is the reward for swiping.
Caption — Static
Tone
Conversational but intentional. Reads like a note from a friend who also happens to see you clearly. Short paragraphs. White space. Never a wall of text.
Opening Line Rule
The first line must work without the rest. If someone only reads one line, it should still land. Lead with the truth — context comes after.

06 — Voice in Practice
Off-Brand vs. On-Brand
The fastest way to understand a brand voice is to see it in contrast. These examples show the same idea — written wrong, then written right.
Off-Brand
"Hustle harder, queen! Your dream life is waiting and you just have to believe in yourself to get there."
On-Brand
"The life you want isn't waiting for more effort. It's waiting for a different version of you — and that's a harder truth to sit with."
Off-Brand
"I went from broke to 6 figures and so can you! Here are the 5 steps I used to manifest my success."
On-Brand
"The strategy was never the problem. I had the steps. What I didn't have yet was the identity to actually follow through on them."
Off-Brand
"Limited spots! Join my program now before it's too late — this is your sign to finally level up!"
On-Brand
"This isn't for everyone. It's for the woman who already knows something needs to change — and is done waiting to feel ready before she moves."
07 — Content Formula
The GMP Framework
Every piece of content — regardless of format — follows this four-part structure. It is not a rigid template. It is a directional flow that ensures content moves from awareness to action.
01
Realization
Name the thing most people haven't said out loud yet. This is the hook — not a question, not a tease, but a truth that creates instant recognition. The reader should feel it before she thinks it.
Example: "Most women aren't stuck because they don't know enough. They're stuck because they haven't seen themselves clearly yet."
02
Explanation
Break down why the realization is true. This is where trust is built. Be specific. Use real patterns, real behavior, real language — not abstract concepts. She should be nodding, not thinking.
Example: "She has the courses. She has the plan. But every time she gets close, she self-sabotages — because the strategy is asking her to show up as a version of herself she hasn't become yet."
03
Embodiment
Show what it looks like when someone actually lives this. A real example, a personal story, or a clear contrast between the old behavior and the new one. This makes the idea tangible, not theoretical.
Example: "When I stopped trying to execute the plan and started asking who I needed to become to hold it — everything changed. Not the strategy. Me."
04
Activation
Leave her with one thing — a question to sit with, a decision to make, or a small action to take. Never a list of five things. One thing. The goal is a shift, not a to-do list.
Example: "The question isn't what's your next step. It's — who do you need to become before that step actually sticks?"
